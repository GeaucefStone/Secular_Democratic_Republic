**Act Number:** ACT-0002  
**Sponsoring Department:** Department of Digital & Financial Integrity  
**Date of Introduction:** [Date]

## PREAMBLE

**WHEREAS** the freedom to transact and communicate is fundamental to a free society;

**WHEREAS** financial institutions and Internet Service Providers serve as essential public conduits, not moral arbiters;

**WHEREAS** organized campaigns to pressure these neutral intermediaries into restricting lawful transactions or communications represent economic coercion and undermine both market freedom and free expression;

**WHEREAS** adults possess the right to use lawful services for lawful purposes without interference from third-party moral advocates;

**BE IT ENACTED** by the Legislative Congress of the Secular Democratic Republic as follows:

---

## Section 1: Short Title

This Act may be cited as the **“Financial & Digital Neutrality Act.”**

## Section 2: Definitions

For the purposes of this Act:

- **“Financial Intermediary”** means any entity that processes payments, extends credit, holds deposits, or facilitates monetary transactions, including but not limited to: banks, credit unions, payment processors (Visa, MasterCard, PayPal, Stripe), and digital wallets.

- **“Internet Service Provider (ISP)”** means any entity that provides access to the Internet, including broadband, mobile data, hosting, or transit services.

- **“Lawful Activity”** means any conduct, transaction, or communication that is legal under the laws of the Republic.

- **“Coercive Advocacy Campaign”** means any organized effort by a non-governmental entity to pressure a Financial Intermediary or ISP into denying service to persons engaged in Lawful Activities.

## Section 3: Neutral Conduit Mandate

1. **No Financial Intermediary or ISP shall deny, restrict, or degrade service to any customer based solely on the lawful nature of the customer’s transactions or communications.**

2. **Financial Intermediaries and ISPs are hereby classified as “Neutral Conduits”** with respect to lawful activity. They shall not:

   - Block, throttle, or prioritize traffic based on content or transaction type

   - Close accounts or refuse service based on moral, political, or ideological disapproval

   - Disclose transaction or browsing data to third parties for advocacy purposes

   - Impose discriminatory terms or fees based on the nature of lawful business

3. **Exceptions:**
   Service denial is permitted **only** if the Intermediary can demonstrate:

   - Actual illegal activity (fraud, money laundering, terrorism financing)

   - Court order or lawful warrant

   - Genuine technical incapacity (e.g., protocol incompatibility)

   - Uniformly applied, content-neutral terms of service (e.g., excessive bandwidth abuse)

## Section 4: Prohibition on Coercive Pressure

1. **It is unlawful for any organization to engage in a Coercive Advocacy Campaign targeting a Neutral Conduit.**

2. **A Neutral Conduit subjected to such pressure may seek a court order** requiring the advocacy group to cease and desist. Violation shall result in statutory damages of no less than ten times the cost of compliance.

## Section 5: Financial Privacy Shield

1. **Financial Intermediaries shall not disclose transaction metadata, account status, or customer identity** related to Lawful Activities to any third party—including advocacy groups, media, or employers—absent a lawful warrant.

2. **ISPs shall not disclose browsing history, connection logs, or traffic data** related to Lawful Activities under the same protections.

## Section 6: Transparency & Accountability Office

1. **There is hereby established the Office of Neutral Conduit Oversight (ONCO)** within the Department of Digital & Financial Integrity.

2. **ONCO shall:**

   - Investigate complaints of service denial

   - Issue binding restoration orders

   - Impose fines of up to 10% of annual domestic revenue for violations

   - Publish annual transparency reports on compliance

## Section 7: Private Right of Action

1. **Any customer harmed by a violation of this Act may sue for:**

   - Actual damages ×10

   - Injunctive relief (service restoration)

   - Attorney’s fees and costs

2. **Class action suits are expressly permitted** for systemic violations.

## Section 8: Whistleblower Protection

Employees of Financial Intermediaries or ISPs who report violations of this Act shall be protected under Article I, Section 20 of the Constitution. Retaliation shall be punishable by personal liability for managers involved.

## Section 9: Penalties

1. **First violation:** Fine of 5% of domestic annual revenue + mandatory service restoration.

2. **Second violation:** Fine of 15% of domestic annual revenue + executive liability.

3. **Third violation:** Possible revocation of domestic operating license.

## Section 10: Extraterritorial Application

This Act applies to any Financial Intermediary or ISP offering services within the Republic, regardless of the company’s country of incorporation.

## JUSTIFICATION FOR CONSTITUTIONAL COMPLIANCE

- **Basis in Article I, Section 3 (Freedom of Expression):** Protects financial and digital expression from de facto censorship.

- **Basis in Article I, Section 8 (Digital Privacy):** Extends privacy to transaction and browsing data.

- **Basis in Article VII (Constitutional Supremacy):** Prevents private intermediaries from creating shadow regulations.

- **Basis in Article XI (National Security):** Ensures financial and digital infrastructure remains neutral and secure.

**Methodology:** This Act follows the law-making pipeline. It identifies a systemic vulnerability (moral coercion of infrastructure), provides a narrowly tailored fix (neutral conduit mandate), and establishes clear enforcement.
