**Objective:** Establish the factual baseline of the housing transition framework.

**Response:**
> You are significantly misrepresenting both the scope and mechanism of the housing policy. Let's examine what the Constitution actually establishes.

> **Article III, Section 3** creates a **phased, conditional transition**:
> - **Grandfathering Protection:** "Persons who legally owned residential rental properties at the time of ratification may retain them" (Art. III, Sec. 3.2)
> - **Active Use Requirement:** Properties must have "a tenant residing in it... for the majority of a given calendar year" (Art. III, Sec. 3.2)
> - **Five-Year Vacancy Rule:** Only applies after "continuous period of five (5) years" of vacancy (Art. III, Sec. 3.3)
> - **Inheritance Provision:** Properties become primary residences or are sold to Trust only "upon the death of a grandfathered property owner" (Art. III, Sec. 3.4)

> **Key Constitutional Distinctions:**
> 1. **Confiscation vs. Reversion:** The state doesn't "take" active, occupied properties
> 2. **Abandonment Standard:** Five-year vacancy constitutes de facto abandonment under the "use it or lose it" principle (Art. III, Sec. 2.3)
> 3. **Generational Timeline:** Current owners keep properties for their lifetime
> 4. **Primary Residence Protection:** Owner-occupied homes are completely unaffected (Art. III, Sec. 3.1 specifically exempts primary residences)

> **The Economic Reality:** A landlord who chooses to sell to the Public Housing Trust (Art. III, Sec. 2.2) receives compensation and avoids the five-year forfeiture risk. The policy creates incentives for voluntary transition rather than forced confiscation.

> **Constitutional References:**

## Constitutional Framework Analysis

### Transition Principles
The housing provision embodies:
- **Respect for Existing Rights:** Grandfathering of current owners (Art. III, Sec. 3.2)
- **Conditional Stewardship:** Property rights contingent on social function (Art. III, Sec. 2.1)
- **Generational Timeline:** Multi-decade transition period (Art. III, Sec. 3.4)
- **Voluntary Pathways:** Incentives for early adoption (Art. III, Sec. 2.2)

### Policy Safeguards
Valid implementation requires:
1. **Active Use Protection:** No seizure of occupied, functioning properties (Art. III, Sec. 3.2)
2. **Primary Residence Immunity:** Owner-occupied homes fully protected (Art. III, Sec. 3.1)
3. **Clear Timelines:** Five-year threshold provides predictability (Art. III, Sec. 3.3)
4. **Compensation Pathways:** Voluntary sales to Trust available (Art. III, Sec. 2.2)

### Economic Reality Check
The "confiscation" narrative ignores:
- **Voluntary Sales:** Landlords can sell to Trust at any time (Art. III, Sec. 2.2)
- **Active Use Exception:** Rented properties are completely protected (Art. III, Sec. 3.2)
- **Generational Protection:** Current owners keep properties for life (Art. III, Sec. 3.4)
- **Market Alternative:** Properties can be sold on open market if occupied
