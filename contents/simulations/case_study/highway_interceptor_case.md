## SYLLABUS

The reasonable belief of an imminent lethal threat, formed during a high-speed confrontation where comprehensive threat assessment is impossible, justifies defensive actions under the Stand Your Ground doctrine. When a motorist observes what appears to be a firearm brandished by a pursuing aggressor, the immediate use of the vehicle as a defensive instrument to neutralize the threat constitutes justifiable defense of self and others, regardless of the object's ultimate authenticity.

---

## OPINION OF THE COURT

**CHIEF JUSTICE STONE** delivered the opinion of the Court.

This case requires us to examine the boundaries of justified defensive force when faced with an apparent lethal threat under conditions that preclude deliberate analysis. We hold that the reasonable perception of imminent danger, not the retrospective forensic certainty of the threat's authenticity, governs the justification for defensive action.

### I. FACTUAL AND PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

On the evening of [Date], respondent Marcus Thorne was traveling northbound on Interstate 77 with his family when he noticed an aggressively operated motorcycle approaching at high speed. The motorcycle, operated by one Jason Miller, drew alongside Thorne's vehicle while Miller made threatening gestures.

According to testimony and traffic camera evidence, Miller then produced a black metallic object that, in the lighting conditions and relative motion, reasonably appeared to be a semi-automatic handgun. Miller waved this object in Thorne's direction while maintaining close proximity at speeds exceeding 85 miles per hour.

Thorne, believing his family faced imminent lethal danger, executed an emergency maneuver, striking Miller's motorcycle and forcing it into the highway shoulder. Miller sustained serious but non-fatal injuries. The object was later determined to be a realistic replica firearm incapable of firing live ammunition.

The State charged Thorne with aggravated assault and reckless endangerment. At trial, Thorne invoked the defense of others under Article I, Section 10 and Article II, Section 9. The trial court convicted, finding that the replica's nature rendered the threat insufficient to justify the force used. The Appellate Division reversed, applying a reasonable perception standard. We granted certiorari.

### II. ANALYSIS

#### A. The "Reasonable Perception" Standard Governs Imminent Threat Assessment

The State contends that the ultimate determination that the object was a replica negates the reasonableness of Thorne's defensive actions. This argument fundamentally misunderstands the nature of imminent threat assessment.

Article II, Section 9 establishes that defensive force is justified when an individual reasonably believes such force is immediately necessary to protect against unlawful force. The test is not whether the threat was objectively real in hindsight, but whether a reasonable person in the same situation would have perceived an imminent lethal threat.

The conditions of this encounter—high speed, limited visibility, rapid development, and the presence of family members—created what this Court recognizes as a "compressed decision environment." In such environments, the law does not require perfect threat assessment, only reasonable perception based on available information.

#### B. The Vehicle as a Defensive Instrument in Mobile Confrontations

Thorne's use of his vehicle as a defensive instrument falls within the protected scope of self-defense under our tiered framework. While the "Cockpit Doctrine" of Article II, Section 9, Clause 5 typically addresses stationary vehicle defense, its principles extend to mobile confrontations where the vehicle becomes both sanctuary and potential trap.

The critical factors supporting the reasonableness of Thorne's actions include:

1. **The Absence of Escape Options:** At highway speeds, with family members present, and an aggressor maintaining pace, the duty to retreat was rendered impossible and unsafe.

2. **The Proportionality of Response:** The controlled collision, while dangerous, represented a measured response compared to the perceived threat of firearm violence.

3. **The Defense of Others:** Thorne acted not only in self-defense but in defense of his passengers, including minor children.

#### C. The Transferred Liability Doctrine Applies to Apparent Threats

Article II, Section 9, Clause 6 establishes that an aggressor who "initiates an unprovoked, violent felony... shall be held legally responsible for any death that occurs as a direct result." While this case involves injury rather than death, the principle extends to the creation of apparent lethal threats.

Miller's decision to brandish what appeared to be a firearm during a high-speed confrontation constituted the initiation of a violent felony—aggravated assault with a deadly weapon. That the weapon was ultimately inoperative does not negate the criminality of his actions nor the reasonableness of the defensive response they provoked.

### III. CONCLUSION

The judgment of the Appellate Division is affirmed. Marcus Thorne's actions constituted justified defensive force under the extreme circumstances he faced.

Our Constitution does not require citizens to gamble with their lives or the lives of their families when faced with what reasonably appears to be imminent lethal threat. The standard remains one of reasonable perception, not retrospective certainty. To hold otherwise would be to empower aggressors with realistic replicas to create lethal scenarios without consequence.

**IT IS SO ORDERED.**

## CONCURRING OPINION

**JUSTICE ELIOTT**, concurring.

I join the Court's opinion in full. I write separately to emphasize the psychological reality of threat assessment under conditions of extreme stress.

The law has long recognized the "reaction time" principle in self-defense jurisprudence. When a police officer faces a split-second decision about a potentially armed suspect, we afford deference to their perception in that moment. The same deference must extend to citizens facing what they reasonably believe to be armed aggression.

The respondent in this case faced not merely a theoretical threat, but a dynamic, high-speed confrontation where milliseconds mattered. His brain processed limited visual data—black metal object, aggressive brandishing, threatening context—and reached the same conclusion most reasonable people would: firearm, imminent danger, defensive action required.

The law cannot demand laboratory certainty in battlefield conditions. The Constitution protects the right to make reasonable defensive decisions, not perfect ones.

## LEGAL ANNEX: The "Compressed Decision Environment" Doctrine

This case establishes the "Compressed Decision Environment" doctrine, which holds that:

1. **Threat assessment** must be evaluated based on the information reasonably available in the moment, not through retrospective analysis.

2. **Reasonable perception** of a lethal threat justifies defensive action, regardless of the threat's ultimate authenticity.

3. **Aggressors** who create such environments through criminal conduct bear responsibility for the defensive actions they provoke.

**Precedent Value:** HIGH  

**Constitutional Impact:** Clarifies Article I, Section 10 and Article II, Section 9 application in dynamic threat scenarios.
